# Classify-stocks-using-K-Means-and-HDBSCAN-clustering-procedure

This study investigates the latent structures of the U.S. equity market by classifying stocks based on their historical return profiles rather than traditional industry sectors. Addressing the question of whether stock clustering is primarily driven by economic sectors or financial risk-return characteristics, we apply unsupervised machine learning techniques to a diversified sample of 22 S&P 500 stocks. Using weekly log returns, we perform Principal Component Analysis (PCA) for dimensionality reduction and compare the clustering performance of the K-Means and Hierarchical Density-Based Spatial Clustering of Applications with Noise (HDBSCAN) algorithms across both 10-year and 5-year horizons.
The empirical findings indicate that stock clustering is predominantly driven by financial risk-return behaviors, such as market sensitivity (Beta), volatility, and risk-adjusted performance (Sharpe ratio), resulting in clusters that reflect distinct investment archetypes rather than traditional sector boundaries. In the 10-year analysis, K-Means slightly outperformed HDBSCAN on traditional validation metrics, as it partitioned defensive assets into compact, spherical clusters, whereas HDBSCAN merged them into a single, continuous density region. Conversely, during the more volatile 5-year period, HDBSCAN achieved superior clustering performance (Silhouette score of 0.790 versus 0.771 for K-Means) by successfully identifying anomalous assets (such as ABBV and XOM) as noise. The study suggests that financial behavior is the primary driver of equity market segmentation, and that density-based clustering models like HDBSCAN offer greater adaptability and robustness during shorter, more volatile market periods.

## Research question
What drives the clustering of stocks? Is it primarily their economic sector or their financial risk-return characteristics?
